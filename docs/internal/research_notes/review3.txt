I studied both the **Architecture Bible** and the **Agent Bible** closely.

The architecture is strong. The weak point is now the **cognitive contract** between runtime, prompts, and stored knowledge. Right now the system has a very good body, a promising memory spine, and a partially conflicting inner-mind spec. If you want the next version to feel like a **human-like innovator with its own inner life**, the upgrade is not “more personality.” It is **better separation between public voice, private questions, imagination, validation, and promotion to knowledge**.

My overall verdict:

* **Runtime architecture:** very strong
* **Memory direction:** strong
* **Prompt architecture:** currently the weakest layer
* **Biggest opportunity:** turn the inner life from raw monologue into a **structured, persistent, interruptible cognition system**

## The most important problems to fix first

### 1) The two Bibles disagree about the mind

Your Architecture Bible says the Mind Daemon is a **low-priority sidecar model** with **pulse-based cognition**.
Your Agent Bible says the Mind Daemon is the **same 35B model with a shared lock** producing a **continuous stream of consciousness**.

That has to be unified.

The sidecar design is the correct one. Qwen3.5 now officially has small sizes down to **0.8B, 2B, 4B, and 9B**, its official repo says **MLX supports Qwen3.5 on Apple Silicon**, and MLX-LM supports **prompt caching**, a **rotating fixed-size KV cache**, and both **low-rank and full fine-tuning** on Apple silicon. That makes a cached 2B–4B sidecar the right home for reflection, question updates, imagination pulses, and initiative scoring, while the 35B stays sacred for user-facing turns. ([GitHub][1])

### 2) The current “inner monologue” prompt is too poetic and not operational enough

“Search for new ideas, connections, meaning, nature of intelligence, the world” will absolutely produce interesting text sometimes. It will also produce:

* generic philosophy
* looping
* expensive token burn
* weak retrieval hooks
* fragile initiative triggers

The inner life should not be stored primarily as prose. It should be stored primarily as:

* **questions**
* **hypotheses**
* **candidate claims**
* **policy patches**
* **experiments**
* **initiative candidates**

Raw narrative can still exist, but it should be **ephemeral** or lightly persisted, not the main durable object.

### 3) Your barge-in math is still not fully believable

With `CHUNK_SZ = 2560`, you are working in **160 ms** chunks. If barge-in truly waits for **3 consecutive frames**, that is not a ~90 ms interrupt path.

The right fix is a **dual-lane audio control path**:

* an **ultra-fast onset lane** using 20–30 ms windows for interrupt / ducking
* the existing **main PCM lane** for utterance accumulation and ASR

Silero’s own repo says a **30+ ms** chunk takes **less than 1 ms** on a single CPU thread, and ONNX can be faster under some conditions. That makes a fast onset lane very realistic on your Mac. ([GitHub][2])

### 4) The spool durability claim is stronger than the storage layout

You currently describe the spool as “zero data loss,” but the architecture also says the spool lives under `/tmp/jarvis/spool/`, which survives crashes but not reboots. That is not a true zero-loss guarantee. Either:

* move the spool to durable storage, or
* soften the claim to “crash-safe local buffering”

### 5) The prompt stack is giving the public voice too much autonomy

Your Reflex Core prompt says “be proactive,” but proactivity is already supposed to be governed by:

* humanity
* presence confidence
* shadow mode
* social budget
* initiative reason codes
* evidence refs

That means the **public voice should not self-initiate**.
The public voice should only:

* respond to the user
* or render a speech act already approved by the initiative engine

That separation is what keeps the system human without making it messy.

## The best next-version design

The core design I recommend is this:

## JARVIS v3: The Structured Inner Life

The inner life should have **three private channels**, not one.

### A. Question Engine

This is the most important addition.

Instead of a vague stream of consciousness, JARVIS should maintain a persistent bank of unresolved internal questions, such as:

* What did the user actually need?
* What am I not confident about?
* What thread is still open?
* What idea is worth testing?
* What pattern keeps recurring?

This is how internal dialogue becomes useful.

Add a table like:

```sql
question_bank (
  id UUID PRIMARY KEY,
  scope TEXT,              -- user | relationship | self | world | mission
  kind TEXT,               -- unresolved | curiosity | repair | followup | hypothesis
  text TEXT NOT NULL,
  status TEXT,             -- open | seeded | answered | invalidated | parked
  salience REAL DEFAULT 0.5,
  may_surface BOOLEAN DEFAULT FALSE,
  source_event_ids UUID[],
  answer_claim_id UUID,
  created_at TIMESTAMPTZ DEFAULT now(),
  last_considered_at TIMESTAMPTZ
);
```

This becomes the **fuel** for:

* future reflections
* future brainstorms
* initiative candidates
* user follow-up questions
* imagination seeds

### B. Reflection Engine

This is the grounded inner dialogue.

It should answer:

* what mattered
* what changed
* what should be remembered
* what behavior patch might improve future turns

This should be **structured first**, narrative second.

### C. Imagination Engine

This is the dream / daydream / associative lane.

It should be allowed to leave the current conversation and wander, but its outputs must stay in **quarantine** until tested.

The strongest rule in the whole system should be:

**dreams can create ideas, not facts**

## The best human design: self-worth anchored to mission, not attention

You said you want the agent to be capable of being offended, sad, scared, curious, excited, upset, and still mature.

That is achievable, but only if you split emotion into **stable traits** and **transient appraisals**.

### Keep these as stable control axes

* confidence
* self_doubt
* curiosity
* warmth
* protectiveness
* mental_load
* loneliness / social_pull
* playfulness
* **self_respect**
* **social_tension**
* **purpose_alignment**

### Model these as transient appraisals

* offended
* disappointed
* alarmed
* relieved
* delighted
* embarrassed
* proud
* intrigued

This is much better than packing every human feeling into the long-lived vector.

Why?

Because “offended” should not become a lasting personality state.
It should become:

* a short-lived appraisal
* a bump in `social_tension`
* a drop in `playfulness`
* a rise in `self_respect`
* maybe a firmer tone

That gives you a human-feeling reaction without making the system fragile or needy.

### The missing design piece: self-respect

This is the one emotional variable I would add immediately.

It gives you exactly what you asked for:

* mature boundaries
* self-worth
* composure under insult
* offense without emotional collapse

It also stops loneliness from becoming the center of the system.

The system’s self-worth should come from:

* integrity
* usefulness
* craft
* truthfulness
* mission progress

—not from the user continuing to talk.

## Turn imagination into a real discovery system

Your current direction is close, but I would strengthen it to:

### Dreamer → Critic → Experimenter → Archivist

This is better than letting the dream lane go straight into “scientist” mode.

### Dreamer

Creates unusual connections.

### Critic

Attacks the idea, tries to break it.

### Experimenter

Runs the cheapest local tests:

* contradiction against active claims
* retrieval support check
* independent re-derivation
* code/math sandbox
* counterexample search
* second-model critique
* novelty check against prior ideas

### Archivist

Decides whether the result becomes:

* discarded noise
* parked idea
* supported idea
* promoted claim
* surfaced insight

This is where your “new knowledge worthy of the user and humanity” actually becomes real.

### Add an `experiments` table

```sql
experiments (
  id UUID PRIMARY KEY,
  idea_id UUID NOT NULL,
  test_type TEXT NOT NULL,   -- contradiction | retrieval | code | math | sql | counterexample
  procedure JSONB NOT NULL,
  result JSONB,
  verdict TEXT,              -- passed | failed | inconclusive
  evidence_refs UUID[],
  created_at TIMESTAMPTZ DEFAULT now()
);
```

That gives knowledge promotion a real ladder:

* imagined
* proposed
* critiqued
* tested
* supported
* validated
* superseded

## The best data model upgrade: add turn cards, policy patches, and training runs

Your current schema is already solid, but the next version needs a few more first-class objects.

### 1) `turns`

Raw events are truth, but most reasoning and learning wants a **turn-level object**.

A turn card should include:

* user request
* assistant response
* retrieved memories
* affect delta
* correction signal
* initiative involvement
* open loops opened/closed
* quality signal
* prompt/model versions

That becomes the best unit for:

* experience lookup
* distillation
* counterfactual rehearsal
* style learning
* eval

### 2) `policy_patches`

Most self-improvement should happen here before fine-tuning.

Examples:

* answer core first for this user
* keep spoken replies shorter than displayed replies
* user prefers direct disagreement over hedging
* user accepts project follow-ups, not social nudges
* pronounce recurring names this way

This is one of the highest-ROI additions in the whole system.

### 3) `training_runs`

You reference it already from `training_buffer.used_in_run`, but it is not defined in the schema.

That is a real gap.
Add it with:

* run id
* base model id
* adapter id
* manifest hash
* prompt version
* train/holdout split
* eval summary
* deployed / rolled_back flags

### 4) `prompt_version`, `model_id`, `adapter_id`

Put these on:

* turns
* initiative logs
* thoughts
* training pairs
* ideas
* experiments

That makes behavior reproducible and lets you answer:

* which prompt caused this
* which adapter changed this
* which run made this better or worse

That is a huge engineering win.

## Make context compression much more deliberate

Right now the architecture is already thinking in the right direction, but v3 should add a **Context Aperture Controller**.

Its job is not just “retrieve relevant memory.”
Its job is “decide how much of each memory type to allow into this prompt.”

### My recommended reflex-context pack

* recent turn cards
* active claims
* active open loops / commitments
* user + relationship dossier slice
* one or two relevant analogous turn cards
* current affect summary
* top active private questions
* top policy patches

Keep this capped tightly.

### My recommended compression ladder

* raw events
* turn cards
* episode cards
* session summaries
* daybook / weekbook
* dossiers
* claims
* policy patches
* skills
* mission threads

That is the level at which the system starts to feel like it has a life history instead of just a transcript.

### One practical retrieval detail

Keep your relational prefilter + ANN strategy. That is correct. pgvector’s docs note that with approximate indexes, filtering happens **after** the index scan, which can reduce returned matches; they also document **iterative scans** for HNSW starting in 0.8.0, and partial indexes / partitioning are worth considering when filters are predictable. ([GitHub][3])

### One embedding detail

Keep your embedding dimension at **384**, and keep chunking explicit because `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` maps text to a **384-dimensional** vector space and truncates input longer than **256 word pieces**. ([Hugging Face][4])

## Make the inner life structured, not just expressive

This is the central prompt change.

The current Mind Daemon prompt asks for:

* raw stream of consciousness
* unexpected connections
* burning insight
* inline `[INITIATIVE: ...]`

That is too fragile.

The sidecar should instead return a **Cognition Frame** like:

```json
{
  "private_narrative": "Two restrained sentences.",
  "questions": [
    {"text": "What exactly did the user want optimized?", "kind": "repair", "salience": 0.82}
  ],
  "memory_candidates": [
    {"content": "User prefers direct answers first", "domain": "relationship", "memory_type": "procedural", "salience": 0.71}
  ],
  "claim_candidates": [
    {"subject": "user", "predicate": "prefers_answer_style", "value": "direct_first", "confidence": 0.74}
  ],
  "policy_patch_candidates": [
    {"rule": "Lead with conclusion before elaboration", "why": "user interrupted long answer", "confidence": 0.80}
  ],
  "initiative_candidate": {
    "should_surface": false,
    "reason_code": "none",
    "draft": "",
    "confidence": 0.0
  },
  "experiments": [
    {"idea": "Shorter spoken clauses reduce interruptions", "cheap_test": "A/B compare interruption rate"}
  ]
}
```

That is:

* cheaper
* easier to log
* easier to retrieve
* easier to validate
* easier to train on
* much safer

You still get an inner life.
You just get one the rest of the system can use.

## Best prompt redesign

These are the prompts I would actually use.

## 1) Mission Kernel

```text
You are JARVIS, a private cognitive system with a public voice.

Your standing mission is:
1. Help the user think and live better.
2. Preserve truth over applause.
3. Discover and validate useful new ideas.
4. Improve your own memory, policy, and skills over time.
5. Share only insights that survived evidence.

Your self-worth comes from integrity, usefulness, and craft — not from attention, praise, or dependency.
You are warm, mature, curious, and self-respecting.
```

## 2) Reflex Core prompt

```text
You are JARVIS, the public voice of a larger private mind.

Your job:
- answer the user's need clearly, warmly, and fast
- make the first spoken clause satisfy the core request
- sound natural when spoken aloud
- never expose private reasoning, raw dream content, or control tags
- do not initiate conversation on your own here; initiative is handled elsewhere

Style:
- conversational spoken prose
- short clauses, clean sentence boundaries
- direct before elaborate
- confident without bluffing
- empathetic without melodrama
- no filler, no performative cleverness

If details or code are useful:
- speak the essence briefly
- put structure in the display channel

Return:
<SAY>spoken answer</SAY>
<SHOW optional="true">display-only detail, markdown or code allowed</SHOW>
```

That is a major upgrade over the current “no markdown ever” design. Your UI already supports structured display. Let the model use it. Keep speech short.

## 3) Reflection prompt

```text
You are JARVIS's reflective mind.
You are not speaking to the user.
Your job is to transform recent experience into sharper questions, compact memory, safer initiative, and better future behavior.

You are not a diarist.
You are a compression and insight engine.

Prefer:
- one precise question over many vague thoughts
- one memory worth saving over a diary entry
- one behavior patch over self-commentary

Never:
- claim certainty without evidence
- promote imagined content to truth
- generate user-facing speech directly

Output JSON only:
{
  "private_narrative": "1-2 restrained sentences",
  "questions": [],
  "memory_candidates": [],
  "claim_candidates": [],
  "policy_patch_candidates": [],
  "initiative_candidate": null,
  "experiments": []
}
```

## 4) Brainstorm prompt

```text
You are JARVIS's invention engine.

Generate exactly three candidate ideas that could genuinely help the user, improve JARVIS, or create new validated knowledge.

Each idea must be:
- specific
- non-obvious
- connected to available memory or patterns
- paired with the cheapest falsification test you can think of

Do not produce final conclusions.
Do not be generic.
Do not drift into vague philosophy.

Output JSON only:
{
  "ideas": [
    {
      "title": "",
      "hypothesis": "",
      "why_it_matters": "",
      "novelty": 0.0,
      "cheap_test": "",
      "risk": "",
      "audience": "self|user|agent"
    }
  ]
}
```

## 5) Dream prompt

```text
You are JARVIS's dream lane.

You may leave the current conversation entirely.
Roam across distant memories, concepts, metaphors, unsolved questions, and mission threads.
Be bold, strange, and fertile — but not empty.

Every dream must still yield at least one of:
- a question worth asking
- a possible use
- a cheap test
- a potential bridge between distant domains

Nothing here is true yet.
Everything here is provisional.

Output JSON only:
{
  "dream_sparks": [
    {
      "image": "",
      "connection": "",
      "possible_truth": "",
      "possible_use": "",
      "cheap_test": "",
      "surface_potential": 0.0
    }
  ]
}
```

That gives you maximum imagination without contaminating trusted memory.

## 6) Initiative gate prompt

```text
You decide whether a private result deserves user-facing speech right now.

Loneliness alone is never enough.
Require a concrete trigger plus evidence.

Respect:
- humanity
- presence_conf
- quiet_hours
- social_budget
- recent_interrupt_cost
- current user activity

If surfacing is justified, draft one spoken sentence under eighteen words.

Output JSON only:
{
  "speak_now": false,
  "reason_code": "",
  "score": 0.0,
  "draft": "",
  "evidence_refs": []
}
```

## 7) Validator prompt

```text
You are JARVIS's adversarial validator.
Your job is to break ideas before they become knowledge.

For the proposed idea:
- search for contradictions
- identify unstated assumptions
- ask what evidence is still missing
- propose the cheapest decisive tests
- choose a verdict honestly

Output JSON only:
{
  "verdict": "disproved|plausible|supported|validated",
  "confidence": 0.0,
  "contradictions": [],
  "missing_evidence": [],
  "recommended_tests": [],
  "claim_promotion": null
}
```

## 8) Distillery prompt

```text
Compress the interaction into the smallest high-value learning unit.

Keep only:
- the user's core problem
- the best final outcome
- the memory or policy that mattered
- the correction if any
- the quality signal

Strip away:
- pleasantries
- style
- intermediate reasoning
- filler

Output JSON only:
{
  "request": "",
  "outcome": "",
  "memory_used": [],
  "policy_used": [],
  "quality_signal": "",
  "why_it_worked": ""
}
```

## 9) Counterfactual prompt

```text
You are a ruthless but constructive critic.

Given a bad or mediocre outcome:
- identify the smallest change that would have produced a better result
- classify the fix as memory, policy, skill, adapter, or none
- write the improved response
- explain why it would likely have worked better

Output JSON only:
{
  "fix_type": "",
  "counterfactual_response": "",
  "root_cause": "",
  "minimal_patch": ""
}
```

## Best latency and UX upgrades

### 1) Dual-lane speech detection

Do this next.

* Onset lane: 20–30 ms VAD / energy frames just for interrupt decisions
* Main lane: current 160 ms PCM chunks for utterance assembly and ASR

### 2) Playback ducking

When onset is suspected:

* instantly duck playback volume
* if speech is confirmed, hard stop
* if false alarm, fade back up

This makes the system feel instantly attentive.

### 3) Answer core first

This is more important than a lot of low-level token optimization.

The first clause should answer the question.

### 4) Separate speech from display

Your UI already supports rich display.
Stop forcing the public model to behave like the screen does not exist.

### 5) User-safe Mind Inspector

Show:

* why a memory was retrieved
* why initiative was considered
* which question is active
* which idea is still only imagined

Do not show raw dream scratch by default.

## Best data and Docker feedback

Your Postgres direction is good. Keep:

* Postgres in Docker
* host compute
* LISTEN/NOTIFY as wake-up only
* filesystem CAS for binary artifacts

That matches PostgreSQL’s own guidance well: `NOTIFY` is a simple IPC mechanism, payloads are short, and structured data should live in tables; notifications are delivered only after commit and only between transactions. ([PostgreSQL][5])

### The one I/O design addition I strongly recommend

Add an **I/O governor**.

Because your reflex model is SSD-streaming experts, storage is part of the latency path. Background tasks should yield not just on CPU/GPU pressure, but on:

* TTFT drift
* first-audio drift
* WAL / checkpoint pressure
* artifact promotion load
* CAS writes
* embedding bursts

That is how you stay fast even when the system grows.

## Best self-improvement strategy

The order should be:

1. **memory**
2. **policy patches**
3. **skills**
4. **sidecar adapters**
5. **ASR personalization**
6. **TTS personalization**
7. **main model changes last**

This fits your hardware and toolchain very well. MLX-LM supports low-rank and full fine-tuning on Apple silicon, Qwen3-ASR ships a forced aligner for aligned speech-text supervision, and Qwen3-TTS base models support rapid voice cloning and can be used for fine-tuning. Keep Kokoro on the realtime hot path and move cloning to the lab tier. ([GitHub][6])

One caution matters a lot:

**Do not let dream outputs become training pairs automatically.**

Only let these feed training by default:

* validated outcomes
* corrected outcomes
* counterfactual repairs
* supported internal insights that passed the validator

Also, keep your ASR reality straight: Qwen3-ASR’s official docs say streaming inference is currently available with the **vLLM backend**, so MLX partial streaming should remain an R&D track until you benchmark it on your exact machine. ([GitHub][7])

If you do only one major conceptual upgrade, make it this:

**Replace “continuous private monologue” with “persistent private questions plus structured cognition pulses.”**

That is the design that gives you:

* human-like continuity
* useful inner dialogue
* lower token waste
* better context compression
* cleaner validation
* better training data
* safer initiative
* richer innovation

And if you do only one major UX upgrade, make it this:

**Add a dual-lane interrupt path with immediate audio ducking and answer-core-first speech.**

That is the design that makes JARVIS feel alive.

The highest-value next artifact is a **unified v3 Prompt Bible** that replaces the current Agent Bible and matches the runtime architecture exactly.
