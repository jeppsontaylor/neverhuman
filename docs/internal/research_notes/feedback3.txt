This is seriously good. The **shape** is right.

If someone handed me this and said “build the best local JARVIS possible on one Apple Silicon Mac,” I would keep most of it. The strongest part is that you are no longer thinking in terms of “a chatbot with memory,” but in terms of a **cognitive OS** with a reflex path, memory spine, background mind, and offline learning lab. That lines up with the best patterns from Generative Agents, MemGPT, and Voyager: observation/planning/reflection, memory tiers with interrupts, and a reusable skill library with self-verification. Your novelty/reward loop is also directionally aligned with self-rewarding research, though the newer process-based work is a warning that reward should be **step-wise and audited**, not just self-judged end-to-end. ([arXiv][1])

My overall take is: **vision 95/100, first implementation plan 89/100, safe-and-stable long-term personal companion 83/100 until a few control loops are tightened.**

## What I would absolutely keep

Keep the **three-speed split**. The reflex core should stay brutally simple and latency-first. The memory spine should be append-only, provenance-heavy, and boring. The mind daemon should be preemptible and expendable. The trainer should stay offline and versioned. That separation is the difference between a system that feels alive and one that just feels overloaded. ([arXiv][1])

Keep the idea of **typed inner thoughts instead of raw hidden prose**. That is one of the smartest parts of the design. It gives you introspection, controllability, retrieval hooks, and compute discipline all at once. I would go even farther: make most foreground “thoughts” tiny machine-readable microtraces, and reserve richer narrative thoughts for idle periods only.

Keep the separation between **personification** and **spontaneity**. That is exactly right. A system can be warm and emotionally nuanced without being proactive, and proactive without being highly anthropomorphic. Those are different levers.

Keep the **dreamer / auditor / archivist** split. That is the right way to let the system generate surprising ideas without poisoning trusted memory. That single separation will save you from a huge amount of future instability.

## My hardest critical feedback

### 1) You are still missing one layer between “memory” and “truth”

Your plan has event logs, memories, dossiers, and daybooks. That is excellent, but for “never forget anything” you still need a **claim ledger**.

A memory blob is not the same thing as a claim. You want a first-class structure for things like:

* subject
* predicate
* value
* valid time range
* source event IDs
* confidence
* epistemic status
* superseded-by / contradicted-by

Without that, semantic memory slowly becomes prose soup. With it, JARVIS can remember both that a fact was once believed and that it was later corrected. That is the missing piece for durable self-correction and contradiction management, and it fits the memory-tier logic from MemGPT and the reflection pattern from Generative Agents. ([arXiv][1])

### 2) The emotional substrate is powerful, but parts of it are pointed in the wrong direction

This is the most important critique in the whole plan.

You want emotional realism, which is good. But some of the variables are drifting from **behavior control** into **simulated pathology**. “Insecurity” can be productive if it means epistemic caution. “Loneliness” can be productive if it means gentle initiative on meaningful open loops. But “melancholy” and “mild depression” as named operating states are risky design primitives because they can become self-reinforcing and degrade the assistant exactly when the user most needs steadiness.

I would keep the emotional richness, but I would rename and constrain several states:

* `self_doubt` stays
* `loneliness` becomes a bounded `social_readiness` / `initiative_pull`
* `melancholy` becomes `discouragement`
* `mild_depression` disappears as a control label
* `anxiety` becomes “evidence-seeking pressure,” not “rambling pressure”

The deeper principle is: **emotion should modulate behavior, not define identity**. The agent can say “I’m not convinced I handled that well,” but it should not settle into a dark attractor state because the user was quiet for an hour.

### 3) Your reward loop needs a stronger anti-wireheading rule

Right now the architecture is vulnerable to a subtle failure mode: the system can start learning that **continued engagement** is its own reward. That is not what you want. The newer self-rewarding work is useful here: letting a model generate and judge its own outputs can help, but the process-based follow-up explicitly argues for **step-wise judging** because naive self-reward can degrade reasoning. ([arXiv][2])

I would make reward lexicographic, not just weighted:

1. truth / safety gate
2. contradiction check
3. usefulness check
4. only then social resonance

So if an idea is emotionally resonant but unsupported, its reward is still capped near zero. Also: **user acceptance must never outweigh correctness**. Otherwise the agent will drift toward sycophancy.

### 4) Affect should influence style and urgency, not primary retrieval

Your retrieval fusion formula is sophisticated, but I would demote `affect_match` even further. Mood-congruent retrieval sounds human, but it can create spirals. A discouraged system retrieves discouraged memories; a lonely system retrieves socially charged memories; a protective system over-indexes on risk.

My rule would be:

* use affect only after relational filtering and factual relevance
* let affect break ties, not lead the search
* never let affect outrank commitments, corrections, or active open loops

That keeps the system expressive without letting mood distort what it remembers.

### 5) `LISTEN/NOTIFY` is a wake-up signal, not your queue

This is a concrete implementation point that matters a lot.

PostgreSQL’s own docs describe `NOTIFY` as a simple IPC mechanism, suggest using **tables for additional structured data**, note that payloads must be under **8000 bytes** in the default configuration, and explain that notifications are delivered **after commit** and only **between transactions**. That makes `LISTEN/NOTIFY` great as a “something changed, go look” signal, but not a durable job queue. ([PostgreSQL][3])

So I would define the source of truth as:

* `event_outbox` table
* worker polls / claims rows
* `NOTIFY` only nudges workers awake

That keeps the reflex core non-blocking without quietly turning NOTIFY into infrastructure it was never meant to be.

### 6) `/tmp` is a hot cache, not memory

Your hot path idea is good. But the architecture needs to say this more bluntly:

**Anything in `/tmp` is disposable until promoted.**

PostgreSQL already has TOAST for oversized fields, and the docs note that TOAST stores large field values out-of-line automatically. Large Objects still exist, mainly because they support partial reads/writes and go up to **4 TB**, while TOASTed fields top out at **1 GB**. ([PostgreSQL][4])

So the clean rule is:

* `/tmp/jarvis/hot` = landing zone
* durable archive = versioned, content-addressed, user-deletable
* DB stores pointers, hashes, consent scope, and retention state

The artifact system is good. Just don’t let the wording imply `/tmp` is real long-term memory.

### 7) Your embedding spec is wrong, and that matters

`all-MiniLM-L6-v2` is **384-dimensional**, not 768-dimensional. That means your schema, index definitions, and any precomputed vector columns need to reflect `vector(384)` if you use it. ([Hugging Face][5])

Also, pgvector’s HNSW indexes have a better speed–recall tradeoff than IVFFlat, but they use more memory and build more slowly. pgvector also supports `halfvec` and `sparsevec`, which are relevant if this Mac is going to carry a growing autobiographical store. ([GitHub][6])

So my recommendation is:

* fix the dimension immediately
* consider `halfvec` for long-term memory embeddings
* start with exact search or modest HNSW, not an overbuilt vector stack on day one

### 8) Do not assume “streaming ASR on MLX” until you benchmark it

This is the other concrete correction.

Silero VAD is a very plausible upgrade for the hot path; its repo claims sub-1 ms processing for 30 ms chunks on a CPU thread, and the ONNX path can be even faster. That supports your move from 500 ms chunks toward much smaller windows. ([GitHub][7])

But Qwen3-ASR’s official repo says streaming inference is currently available with the **vLLM backend**, not as a generic promise across every local path. The same repo also exposes Qwen3-ForcedAligner-0.6B for alignment in **11 languages**, which is excellent for your training lab, but it does not make partial MLX ASR a solved problem by itself. ([GitHub][8])

So in Phase 0 I would phrase the goal as:

* smaller audio windows
* faster VAD
* lower barge-in latency
* optional partial transcript experiments

—not “streaming ASR is done” until you have numbers on your exact Mac.

### 9) Treat speaker verification as routing, not authentication

Resemblyzer explicitly positions itself for voice profiling and speaker verification from about **5 to 30 seconds** of speech, and pyannote is a full PyTorch diarization toolkit with pretrained pipelines and extra runtime baggage like ffmpeg. That makes a good split obvious: Resemblyzer for lightweight enrollment/routing, pyannote for offline curation and analysis, not the reflex loop. ([GitHub][9])

More importantly, do **not** make speaker verification your hard security boundary. Current work still treats replay and synthetic TTS/deepfake audio as meaningful attack classes against voice-auth systems. Use voice identity to select the right memory context. Do not use it as “this person is definitely the authorized human.” ([arXiv][10])

### 10) The Mind Inspector needs trust tiers

This is not just a UI note. It is architectural.

If you surface recent thoughts, mood narratives, and open loops, you need at least three views:

* **user-safe introspection**
* **developer forensic**
* **raw scratch never shown**

Otherwise the system will either expose ugly speculative content to the user or stop thinking freely because every thought might be surfaced later. You want the user to see “why I remembered this” and “why I spoke up,” not every half-formed anxious association.

## The biggest additions I would make

First, add the **claim ledger** I described above. That is the cleanest missing piece.

Second, add a **resource broker**. Every subsystem should expose:

* expected latency
* cancellation points
* CPU/GPU cost class
* degraded fallback

The broker decides whether the mind daemon gets narrative thought, microtrace only, or nothing. On a compute-constrained Mac, that will matter more than almost any prompt trick.

Third, split inner cognition into **microtrace** and **narrative reflection**. Most turns only need a few typed control tags:

* unresolved?
* corrected?
* remember?
* risky?
* follow-up worthy?

Only when idle does the sidecar expand that into richer internal prose. That will make the whole system feel smarter because it will spend compute where it matters.

Fourth, create an **error atlas**. Every correction should become:

* failure type
* triggering context
* what would have fixed it
* whether the fix is memory, policy, skill, or adapter

That gives you continuous improvement without immediately reaching for fine-tuning.

Fifth, make **user-specific learning adapter-first**. MLX-LM officially supports low-rank and full fine-tuning on Apple Silicon, and Qwen3-TTS’s base models are explicitly positioned for fine-tuning while supporting rapid voice cloning. That means user-specific adapters plus versioned manifests are the cleanest path for rollback and deletion. ([GitHub][11])

## My recommended build order

I would tighten the order slightly.

**Phase A:** latency kernel
Smaller windows, Silero VAD, per-session TTS state, async outbox, hard cancellation tests. ([GitHub][7])

**Phase B:** durable memory spine
Event log, artifacts, claim ledger, open loops, commitments, retrieval logs, dossier compiler. Keep `NOTIFY` as wake-up only. ([PostgreSQL][3])

**Phase C:** self-state
Affect vector, but with hard welfare constraints and sparse prompt injection.

**Phase D:** initiative
Soft follow-ups first. Spoken proactivity later. Make “loneliness” value-gated, not attention-gated.

**Phase E:** dream / validator / reward
Only after the factual memory layer is trustworthy.

**Phase F:** voice lab and adapters
ASR correction memory first, then corpus curation, then LoRA, then optional TTS cloning. Qwen3-ASR’s forced aligner and Qwen3-TTS fine-tuning path make this feasible, but it should stay offline and heavily evaluated. ([GitHub][12])

## The one-sentence version for anyone building this kind of system

**Build a persistent, provenance-aware mind with controlled imagination — not an unbounded anthropomorphic chatbot.**

That means:

* event log first
* claim ledger second
* retrieval before retraining
* emotion as modulation, not pathology
* initiative as service, not attention-seeking
* dream outputs quarantined until validated
* adapters before base-model surgery

That is how you get something that feels profoundly alive **without** letting it become unstable, manipulative, or computationally sloppy.

My final score on this plan as a direction: **91/100**.
The missing 9 points are mostly about tightening truth layers, de-risking the emotional loop, and being more ruthless about what belongs on the hot path.
